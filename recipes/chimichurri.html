Chimichurri

Or

How to impress people with minimal effort

We've all been there; you've been invited to grill with some friends but you don't want to shell out your hard-earned cash on expensive steaks and you certainly don't want to be the chump who brings unseasoned chicken... so what's the move???

The answer is Chimichurri; it goes with anything grilled, from fish to bread, costs about $5 to make, and is sure to impress anyone, both for its culinary prowess and the cultural clout you'll accumulate by bringing in such a staple of Southern Cone cuisine.

What's this operation gonna take?

  • A generous ammount of Olive Oil (single origin, extra virgin if possible)
  • A little Red Wine Vinegar
  • A bunch of Parsley (Italian or Curly will do)(by "a bunch" I mean either a single unit sold in a bunch, or the figurative concept of "a whole bunch of parsley" depending on how much you need to make and how much you like parsley.
  • 2-5 cloves of Garlic
  • Salt and Pepper to taste
  • Crushed Red Pepper
  • sage (optional)
  • Thyme (optional)
  • Red Bell Pepper (optional)

Now for the fun part

Mince your parsley and garlic, the ratio should be determined by how much you like either ingredient. You can do this in a food processor or by hand. I personally prefer by hand because the texture is a bit more rough and traditional.

With these two items minced, you'll toss them in a bowl or tupperware, flatten them across the bottom and cover them with olive oil; please don't ask how much, just cover them. You can't have too much olive oil.

If you have elected to use the optional ingredients, good for you. You'll want to add those now. I recommend going light on the sage as it is rather bitter, and light on the Red Bell Pepper as it should not overpower the other flavors.

With that complete, add your salt and pepper, and just a few splashes of red wine vinegar.

Proceed to mix all ingredients together and give it a try. It will likely be rather sharp, feel free to add a pinch of sugar to fight this and adjust any other ingredients as needed.

Let your lovely Chimi rest at room temperature for some time, ideallly overnight, but a few hours will do. This is what dulls that initial sharpness. The garlic needs time to absorb some of the vinegar and Oil to calm itself down.

The last step of course is to show off this dish to your friends and await the praise for such a unique offering. Do not under any circumstances admit that you found this on the internet. You of course need to imply that you got it from a friends grandmother or aunt or some such vaguery to add a bit of story to the dish because, let's face it, you brought a garnish to a cook-out...You're still a chump.
